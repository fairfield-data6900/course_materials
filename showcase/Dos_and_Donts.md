# Presenting Like a Consultant — Behavioral Guide

**For:** All teams presenting at the May 7 Showcase
**The rule:** You're not presenting a school project. You're delivering a solution to a client.

**The goal:** Win a follow-up conversation. Every person in that room is either a current client or a potential one. Your 25 minutes on stage is the audition — the real work happens in the conversation after.

---

## Dress Code: Business Casual

| Yes | No |
|:----|:---|
| Collared shirt, blouse, or clean sweater | Hoodies, graphic tees, athleisure |
| Chinos, slacks, clean dark jeans | Sweatpants, shorts, ripped jeans |
| Clean sneakers, loafers, flats | Slides, flip-flops |
| Simple accessories | Distracting hats or sunglasses |

**The test:** Would you wear this to a job interview at a consulting firm? If no, change.

---

## The First 10 Seconds

The audience decides whether to pay attention before you finish your first sentence. Make it count.

| Do | Don't |
|:---|:------|
| Walk to the front with purpose | Shuffle up apologetically |
| Make eye contact with the room before speaking | Start talking while still looking at your laptop |
| Open with the problem, not your name | Open with "So, um, our project is called..." |
| Speak your first sentence from memory | Read your first sentence off a slide |

**Template:** "Imagine you're a [persona] and every week you have to [painful task]. That's the problem we solved."

---

## Room Presence

### Where to Stand
- Next to the screen, not behind the laptop
- Angled 45° toward the audience so you can gesture at the screen without turning your back
- Non-presenters: seated together to the side, facing the audience, attentive — not on phones

### What to Do with Your Hands
- Rest at your sides or gesture naturally when making a point
- Hold a pen or clicker if you need something to anchor to
- Don't: cross arms, hands in pockets the whole time, fidget with jewelry or hair

### Eye Contact
- Pick three spots in the room: left, center, right. Rotate between them
- Talk to people, not to the screen and not to the floor
- During Q&A: look at the person who asked, then scan the room as you answer

---

## Demo Narration

The demo is 10 minutes. The audience can't read your screen from across the room. Your voice is the demo.

| Do | Don't |
|:---|:------|
| "Watch what happens when I run this" — then pause while it executes | Click frantically in silence |
| "Notice the output here — it generated five clips ranked by score" | "So yeah, it did the thing" |
| Point at the screen and read key results aloud | Assume they can see the 11pt font on your terminal |
| Narrate the value: "This step used to take 2 hours manually" | Narrate the code: "Here I'm calling the analyze function with these parameters" |

**The golden rule:** WHAT it does, not HOW it's built.

### When Something Breaks

It will happen. It happens to senior consultants too. What matters is recovery, not prevention.

| Severity | Script |
|:---------|:-------|
| **Minor glitch** | "Let me try that again." (Retry calmly. No apology needed.) |
| **Takes more than 10 seconds** | "While that loads, let me tell you what you'd see here." (Switch to narration.) |
| **Total failure** | "We have a technical issue — let me walk you through the output using our backup." (Switch to screenshots. No panic.) |

**Never say:** "This worked five minutes ago, I swear."

## Q&A Composure

### The Pause

When someone asks a question, do not start talking immediately. Take one breath. This does three things: it stops "um," it shows confidence, and it gives you time to think.

### Answer Structure: Answer → Example → Stop

1. **Answer** the question directly in one sentence
2. **Give one example** or piece of evidence
3. **Stop talking.** Don't keep going until you run out of things to say.

Most students lose credibility not by giving wrong answers but by rambling until they say something wrong.

### Handling Specific Scenarios

| Scenario | What to say | What NOT to say |
|:---------|:------------|:----------------|
| You know the answer | "[Direct answer]. For example, [evidence]." | A five-minute explanation |
| You don't know | "I don't have that answer right now. I'll follow up after the session." | "Umm... I think maybe..." (guessing) |
| Out of scope | "That's outside our V1 scope. We've documented it as a future improvement." | "We didn't think of that" |
| They challenge your approach | "That's valid. We chose this because [reason]. The trade-off is documented in our HANDOFF." | Getting defensive or arguing |
| They ask about something that failed | "We encountered that issue. Here's how we resolved it / here's our mitigation plan." | "Yeah that was a problem, we ran out of time" |

### Team Coordination During Q&A

- **PM fields first.** If it's technical, hand off: "Great question — [Name] built that component. [Name]?"
- **Don't talk over each other.** One voice at a time.
- **Non-answerers:** nod, stay engaged, face the audience. Don't check your phone.
- **If a teammate is struggling:** support, don't rescue. Let them finish, then add: "I'd also add that..."

## The Last 10 Seconds

End as strong as you started. Don't trail off.

| Do | Don't |
|:---|:------|
| "That's our solution. We'd love to explore how this could work in your organization. We're happy to answer any questions." | "So... yeah... that's pretty much it, I guess." |
| PM closes with a clear sentence and eye contact | Everyone looks at each other wondering who's supposed to talk |
| Thank the audience after Q&A: "Thank you for your time and questions. We'll be around afterward if you'd like to continue the conversation." | Pack up while the last question is being answered |

## Before and After the Stage

Your 25 minutes on stage is not the whole showcase. The conversations before and after are where real opportunities happen.

### Before You Present

| Do | Don't |
|:---|:------|
| Arrive early. Introduce yourself to guests as they arrive: "Hi, I'm [Name] from [Team]. We're presenting [topic] today." | Huddle with your team in the corner until it's your turn |
| Ask guests what they do: "What's your role? What brings you to the showcase?" | Launch into your project pitch unprompted |
| Listen more than you talk — learn what problems they care about | Ignore the audience until you're on stage |
| Sit in the front rows so you're visible and engaged during other presentations | Sit in the back scrolling through your notes |

### After You Present

This is the most important 15 minutes of the entire showcase for your team.

| Do | Don't |
|:---|:------|
| Step off stage and walk toward the audience, not away from them | Retreat to your seats and decompress |
| Approach anyone who asked a good question: "You raised a great point about [topic]. I'd love to hear more about how that plays out in your work." | Wait for people to come to you |
| Exchange contact info — LinkedIn, email, business card | Assume they'll remember you tomorrow |
| PM follows up within 24 hours: a short email thanking them for attending + offering to continue the conversation | Let the connection go cold |

### The Follow-Up Email (PM sends within 24 hours)

> Subject: Great meeting you at the AI & Tech Institute Showcase
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for attending the showcase yesterday and for your question about [topic]. Our team enjoyed the conversation.
>
> I've attached our HANDOFF document with the full technical details. If [solution] could be useful for [their context], we'd welcome the chance to explore that further.
>
> Best regards,
> [PM Name]
> [Team Name] — AI & Tech Institute, Fairfield Dolan

### What NOT to Do After Presenting

- Don't check your phone the moment you're done
- Don't leave early — stay for every team's presentation and clap like you mean it
- Don't debrief loudly with your team about what went wrong while guests are in the room

## The 5 Credibility Killers

Avoid these and you're ahead of 90% of student presentations:

1. **Reading from the slide.** If the audience can read it, you don't need to say it. Add something the slide doesn't show.
2. **"Um" filling.** Silence is better than "um." Pause. Breathe. Speak.
3. **Apologizing for your work.** "It's not perfect but..." — never say this. Present what you built with confidence.
4. **Turning your back to the room.** Stand beside the screen, not behind the laptop.
5. **Uncoordinated handoffs.** Practice who speaks when. "Now I'll hand it to [Name] for the demo" is planned. Awkward silence while teammates figure out whose turn it is — is not.

---

## One Week Before: Practice Drill

Each team member, alone, in front of a mirror or camera:

1. Say your opening sentence out loud without looking at anything
2. Explain what your skill does in 30 seconds to someone who's never seen it
3. Answer this question out loud: "Why should I care?"

If you can do all three without "um," without looking away, and without apologizing — you're ready.
